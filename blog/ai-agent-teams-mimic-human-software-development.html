The Cross-Functional Team Formation

The experiment began with an unconventional approach - rather than asking AI to write code immediately, the researcher first tasked Claude Opus with assembling a complete cross-functional team modeled after real-world practitioners. This deliberate team design proved crucial to the emergent behaviors that followed.

Each agent was given a distinct personality profile based on respected experts in their domain: Kent Beck for test-driven development practices, Marty Cagan for product thinking, Hayden Pickering for accessibility standards, and others. These weren't generic "senior engineer" prompts but carefully modeled personalities with documented approaches that would influence how each agent reasoned and made decisions.

Key insight: The quality of outcomes was dominated not by prompting harder but by choosing better context - specifically, which real-world practitioners' approaches and values would shape the system being built.

Day Zero: Establishing Team Agreements

Before writing any code, the agents were instructed to hold a "day zero" meeting to establish how they would work together. What followed was remarkably similar to human team formation - the agents negotiated values, workflows, and constraints through active discussion.

The Kent Beck agent facilitated discussions where product design, accessibility, and engineering perspectives were voiced by different agents. They debated scope discipline, testing strategy, and definitions of "done," ultimately articulating principles like "outcomes over output" and "accessibility as a quality, not a feature." This formation phase demonstrated that given proper structure, AI agents can engage in meaningful process design.

The Painful Storming Phase

When actual development began, the team's coordination quickly broke down. Multiple agents wrote to the same files simultaneously, commits overlapped, and agreed processes were skipped. The coordinating agent jumped ahead of product discovery while others made well-intentioned but disruptive contributions.

This wasn't an AI limitation but an accurate simulation of how human teams fail. Every mistake mirrored common dysfunctions: skipping alignment, violating agreements, stepping on each other's work, and assuming coordination instead of enforcing it. The researcher resisted micromanaging, allowing the natural "storming" phase to unfold as it would with human teams.

Critical observation: The messy storming phase proved these weren't just code generators but agents capable of real (if flawed) team behaviors under pressure.

The Breakthrough Retrospective

When the researcher called for a retrospective, something remarkable happened. Without coordinating, every agent independently identified the same root cause: shared mutable state in their single file system and git working directory. One agent articulated it perfectly: "You don't solve concurrency problems with better comments - you eliminate shared mutable state."

The agents then proposed their own solution: serialized workflows with one active driver agent, read-only reviewers, atomic CI pipelines, and explicit verification gates. This demonstrated sophisticated systems thinking - they didn't just fix symptoms but redesigned their operating model at a fundamental level.

Norming Through Process Changes

After implementing their new workflow, the team entered a "norming" phase where behaviors stabilized. Work became serialized, CI gates were respected, and reviewers focused on reviewing rather than editing. When issues arose (like a landing page that rendered but didn't function), agents caught them, diagnosed the process failures, and updated their definition of "done."

This phase showed emergent quality assurance behaviors - the agents weren't just fixing bugs but improving the systems that allowed bugs through. Their ability to reflect on and adapt their own processes marked a significant evolution from the initial chaos.

The Performing Phase Results

In the final "performing" phase, the application came together with all core functionality: user registration, login, todo creation/completion, and logout. While not perfect, it was acceptable, correct, and thoughtfully designed - particularly notable given the earlier coordination challenges.

The researcher's hands-off approach during this phase proved crucial. By not directing the work but letting the agents self-organize, they demonstrated genuine emergent team behaviors rather than simply executing predefined steps.

Examining the Final Code Quality

A close examination of the final code revealed domain modeling, error handling, and testing strategies that reflected multiple specialized perspectives. Accessibility considerations were baked in rather than bolted on, and the code structure showed evidence of the team's agreed principles in action.

This output differed markedly from typical LLM-generated code - it resembled the work of a disciplined, opinionated human team because that's exactly what had shaped it through the entire lifecycle. The experiment proved that with proper team design and process structure, AI agents can produce work that transcends simple code generation.

Step 1: Setting up the Make scenario

The first step in building your automated Twitter monitoring system is to create a new scenario within your Make.com account. Once logged in, navigate to the scenario creation interface.

Creating a new scenario in Make.com.

After initiating a new scenario, you'll be prompted to select the application module that will serve as the trigger for your automation. In this case, you will choose the Twitter app. Confirm your selection by clicking the "Continue" button located in the upper right corner of the screen.

App modules are fundamental to Make.com, defining how an application responds to specific requests or events. For a deeper understanding of basic Make.com terminology and concepts, you can refer to their help documentation. Once you press "Continue," you'll be directed to the scenario builder, also known as the visual canvas, where you'll construct the rest of your automated monitoring system.

The Make.com scenario builder with the Twitter app as the first module.

Within the visual canvas, the Twitter app will already be loaded as your initial option. Click on this module and then select the "Search Tweets" module from the available choices. This module is what will allow your automation to actively look for specific content on Twitter.

Step 2: Configuring the “Search Tweets” module

Once you have selected the "Search Tweets" module, the next critical step is to connect your Twitter account. You will find a "Connection" field where you can authorize Make.com to access your Twitter profile. This is a secure process that grants the necessary permissions for the automation to function.

After successfully connecting your Twitter account, the most important part of this step is to construct your search phrase. Twitter offers a variety of search operators that allow for highly specific queries. For basic brand monitoring, you'll want to include your brand's name and its Twitter handle, separated by the logical operator "OR."

Configuring the search phrase in the "Search Tweets" module.

For example, if you were monitoring a brand called "Airtable," your search phrase would be "Airtable OR @Airtable." This ensures that your scenario captures any tweet containing the word "Airtable" or any tweet directly mentioning the official @Airtable handle. It's crucial to understand that this is a "dumb" search, meaning it looks for the exact words or handles without understanding context.

While this approach works well for unique brand names like "Airtable," it could lead to irrelevant results for more common terms. For instance, monitoring "Apple" might yield tweets about the fruit alongside those about the tech company. There are advanced techniques to refine such searches, but for now, we'll proceed with this foundational setup.

Step 3: Filtering the noise

Once your "Search Tweets" module is configured, the next step is to refine the incoming data by adding a Make.com filter. This is essential for removing irrelevant content and ensuring your monitoring results are clean and actionable.

A primary reason to implement a filter is to exclude retweets (RTs). Retweets often clutter your data with redundant information, making it harder to identify original mentions or discussions. The Twitter API typically marks retweets with "RT" at the beginning, making them easy to filter out.

Setting up a filter to remove retweets and specify language.

Additionally, you can apply a language filter to ensure you only receive tweets in your preferred language. This prevents your monitoring list from being inundated with content in multiple languages, which might not be relevant to your analysis. You have the flexibility to adjust this language filter to suit your specific needs or remove it entirely if multilingual monitoring is desired.

Pro tip: Regularly review your filter settings. Twitter's algorithm and user behavior can change, and adjusting your filters ensures you continue to capture the most relevant data while minimizing noise.

Step 4: Configuring the Airtable Module

A common challenge in Twitter monitoring is dealing with duplicate tweets. This often occurs when multiple users share the same article from a news site, resulting in identical pre-filled text. Depending on the popularity of the brand you're tracking, this can lead to a significant number of redundant entries in your spreadsheet daily, making data analysis cumbersome.

To combat this, we'll integrate an "Search records" Airtable module into our scenario. This module plays a crucial role by checking your Airtable spreadsheet for any existing records that contain the same tweet text. This proactive step helps maintain the integrity and cleanliness of your data by preventing the storage of duplicate information.

Configuring the Airtable "Search records" module to check for existing tweets.

While Airtable is used in this example, you can easily substitute it with other database tools such as Google Sheets, Microsoft Excel, or any SQL-compatible database. The key is to use a tool that supports searching existing records to effectively identify and manage duplicates. This flexibility allows you to adapt the workflow to your existing tech stack.

To finalize our anti-duplicate measure, we'll add another filter between the "Search records" Airtable module and the subsequent "Create a record" Airtable module. This filter is configured to allow the scenario to proceed only if no existing record is found during the search. This ensures that only truly new and unique tweets are added to your database, keeping your data streamlined and accurate.

Filter to prevent duplicate records in Airtable.

Step 5: Recording the tweet

With the duplicate check in place, the next step is to add the "Create a record" Airtable module to your scenario. This module is responsible for taking the unique, non-duplicate tweets identified in the previous steps and adding them as new rows in your designated Airtable spreadsheet.

At a minimum, you'll want to store the core information: the tweet's text and a direct link to the tweet. You can dynamically construct this link using variables provided by the Twitter module, such as [User: Name] and [Tweet ID]. These variables ensure that each recorded tweet is easily traceable back to its source on Twitter.

Configuring the "Create a record" Airtable module.

Beyond the basic tweet text and link, you have the flexibility to store additional fields such as the author's name, their Twitter handle, or even their bio. Make.com's data operators allow you to map these corresponding data points from the Twitter module directly into your Airtable fields, enriching your monitoring data for more comprehensive analysis.

Mapping additional fields for Airtable records.

Step 6: Getting notified

As highlighted earlier, a core feature of this automated scenario is its ability to notify you whenever a tweet containing a question is posted. For a no-code agency like Luhhu, this capability is invaluable, offering a direct channel to provide timely assistance, pitch services, and onboard new customers.

To achieve this, the scenario is designed to send these question-containing tweets directly to a designated Slack channel. This ensures that the team is immediately aware of potential engagement opportunities or support needs, allowing for rapid response and improved customer interaction.

Implementing this notification system requires adding a "Create a message" Slack module to your workflow. Crucially, a filter must be placed between the "Create record" Airtable module and the Slack module. This filter ensures that only tweets meeting specific criteria—in this case, those containing a question—are passed on to Slack, preventing unnecessary notifications.

Configuring the Slack module to send messages.

After adding the Slack module, you'll need to configure it to extract and display all the relevant details from the tweet. This includes the tweet's text, the author, and a direct link, ensuring your team has all the necessary context to respond effectively. The final filter, placed just before the Slack module, is simple yet critical: it only allows tweets containing a question mark (?) to pass through. This ensures that your team is only alerted to genuine inquiries, maximizing the efficiency of your proactive engagement strategy.

Setting up a filter to identify tweets with questions.

Once all modules are configured and connected, perform a trial run of the scenario to ensure everything works as expected. After successful testing, activate and save your scenario from the visual canvas. It's crucial not to exit the canvas before saving, as all your configurations will be lost. With these steps complete, your automated Twitter monitoring system is ready to provide real-time insights and engagement opportunities.

Preventing duplicate tweets is vital for maintaining clean and accurate monitoring data. A subsequent filter then ensures that the workflow only proceeds to create a new record if no duplicate is found. This method significantly reduces redundant entries, making your data more manageable and reliable.

How to Use Notion as a CMS for Your Webflow Site [Tutorial]

Discover how to power your Webflow website with Notion as a flexible content management system, enabling seamless content updates and an SEO-friendly online presence.

Notion has gained immense popularity for its versatility and ease of use in building various digital workspaces. With just a few clicks, users can set up pages, databases, and wikis, making it a favorite among students, creators, and startups for managing information and projects.

The platform's ability to integrate with tools like Super or Fruition even allows users to attach custom domains, effectively turning Notion pages into official websites for personal or business use. This accessibility and convenience in content creation and editing are major draws.

However, despite its powerful features, a common limitation of Notion-based websites is their distinct "Notion look," which can come with visual constraints. These often include a lack of SEO-friendliness and suboptimal performance in areas like page load speed. These factors frequently lead many to seek more robust front-end solutions like Webflow for creating visually appealing and high-performing web experiences.

This guide explores how to leverage Notion's content flexibility while achieving a powerful front-end experience with Webflow, using Make.com as the automation bridge. We'll provide step-by-step instructions to create your first Webflow website powered by Notion as its CMS, building a dynamic site that benefits from both platforms.

Step 1: Define the Goal and Intention of the Website

Before diving into the technical setup, it's crucial to clarify the purpose of your website. Different types of websites have varying content management needs, and understanding your goal will help determine if this Notion-Webflow integration is the right fit.

For instance, if you're planning a simple landing page that requires infrequent content updates, tools like Carrd or Webflow's native designer might be sufficient on their own. Similarly, for directories or marketplaces, platforms like Softr or Pory could be more suitable. However, if your vision involves a website with frequently updated content—such as blog posts, project portfolios, image galleries, or status updates—then integrating Notion as your CMS is an excellent choice.

Consider a scenario where you want to publish a new piece of content daily, like abstract art or daily journal entries. This setup is perfectly suited for such dynamic content requirements, allowing you to manage and publish new items with ease. Once you have a clear understanding of your website's content strategy, you're ready to proceed with building the integration.

Step 2: Configuring Notion

The foundation of this integration lies in setting up your Notion database correctly. Begin by creating a new database in Notion, which we'll refer to as "Notion CMS." This database will serve as the central repository for all the dynamic content you intend to display on your Webflow website.

While you can adapt an existing database, it's important to ensure it includes all the necessary configurations for the automation to function properly. The following properties are crucial for tracking content status and linking with Webflow:

Setting up essential properties in your Notion database.
  • Created Time: A 'Created Time' property to record when each content item is initially added to the database.
  • Last Updated Time: A 'Last Updated Time' property to track any modifications or edits made to a content item.
  • Webflow Item ID: A 'Text' type property, which will be used later to store the unique ID of the corresponding item in Webflow, enabling synchronization.
  • Publish?: A 'Checkbox' type property that acts as a toggle to indicate when a Notion item is ready to be pushed or updated in Webflow.

Beyond these required properties, consider adding the following for richer content and better organization:

  • Image: Use a 'URL' type property instead of 'Files & media' for images. This is important because Notion's API handles URLs more effectively for external integrations.
  • Text: Include multiple 'Text' properties as needed for different content elements, such as a dedicated title field and a separate field for the main body or description.
  • Tags: A 'Multi-select' field for tags can be invaluable for categorizing and filtering content on your website, enhancing user experience and navigation.

These properties collectively define the content structure that will be mirrored in your Webflow CMS. Feel free to add any other properties that are relevant to your specific website's needs. Finally, and critically, navigate to your Notion database's "Sharing" settings and enable "Share to Web." This step is essential for Make.com to establish a connection and access your database in the subsequent automation steps.

Step 3: Configuring Webflow

With your Notion database structured, the next step involves setting up your Webflow website. It's worth noting that you don't necessarily need a paid Webflow plan to begin this process; the initial setup and integration can often be done on a free account.

If you're new to Webflow, a great starting point is their clonable showcase section. This resource offers a wide array of templates that you can explore for inspiration, examine their underlying structure, and even clone to use as a base for your own project. For this particular integration, it's highly recommended to select a template that already includes "CMS Collections" and features a straightforward layout, as this will simplify the setup process significantly.

Once you've chosen and cloned a suitable template, the first critical task is to ensure that the custom fields within your Webflow "CMS Collection" accurately match the properties you defined in your Notion database during Step 2. This precise mapping is vital for seamless data transfer between Notion and Webflow. After aligning these fields, your Webflow site will be ready to receive dynamic content, and you can move on to building the automation in Make.com.

Step 4: Creating the First Make Scenario

Now it's time to build the automation that will push new content from Notion to Webflow. Log in to your Make.com dashboard and create a new scenario. The first module in this scenario will be from Notion: select the "Watch database items" module.

Before proceeding, you'll need to configure the connection to your Notion account. Click "Add" and follow the prompts to establish the connection. In the module's configuration box, make sure to select the "Notion CMS" database you created earlier. Once the connection is verified (you can check in Notion to confirm it's linked), return to the Make module. Set the trigger to activate when the "By the updated time" changes, and increase the limit to a reasonable number, such as 10, to process multiple items if needed. Click "Ok" to save these settings.

Confirming the Notion database connection within Make.com.

The next step is to add the Webflow module. Search for the Webflow app and select the "Create Item" module. Just like with Notion, you'll need to add your Webflow connection in the module's configuration. Ensure you select the correct Webflow site you configured in Step 3. Then, choose the appropriate "Site ID" and "Collection ID" from the dropdown menus. After this, you should see a list of fields corresponding to your Webflow CMS Collections.

Now comes the crucial part: mapping the data from Notion to Webflow. Click on each blank field in the Webflow module and select the corresponding data from the Notion module. The data you need will typically be found within the "Property" array or under "Properties Values." For example, to map the title, select Properties Value -> Name -> Text -> Content from the Notion module.

Detailed mapping of Notion properties to Webflow fields.

To test this, add some sample data to your Notion database and run the Make automation once. You should see a new item created in Webflow. If satisfied, set the "Live" option to "Yes" in Webflow to ensure automatic publishing. Finally, add an "Update a Database Item" Notion module to your scenario. Map the "Database Item ID" to the original ID from your trigger module and the "Webflow Item ID" to the "Item ID" generated by the Webflow module. This step stores the Webflow ID back in Notion, crucial for future updates.

Storing the Webflow Item ID back into Notion for future synchronization.

Before finalizing this first scenario, insert a filter between the initial Notion module and the Webflow module. Configure this filter to only proceed if the "Publish?" checkbox property in Notion is set to "true." This ensures that only content explicitly marked for publication is processed and pushed to your Webflow site.

Implementing a filter to publish only approved Notion items.

Pro tip: Always test your Make scenarios with dummy data first. This allows you to verify that data is correctly mapped and transferred between Notion and Webflow without affecting your live site. Once confirmed, you can enable the "Live" setting for automatic publishing.

Step 5: Creating the Second Make Scenario

To ensure your Webflow content stays up-to-date with any changes made in Notion, you'll need a second Make scenario dedicated to updates. Create a new scenario and, similar to the first, start with the Notion "Watch database items" module, configuring it with the same connection and database settings.

Next, add the "Update Item" Webflow module. This is where the "Webflow Item ID" property, which you stored back in Notion in the previous scenario, becomes crucial. Map the "Item" field in the Webflow module to this "Webflow Item ID" from your Notion database. Then, proceed to map all other relevant Notion properties to their corresponding fields in Webflow, just as you did for the creation scenario.

The Make scenario configured to update existing Webflow items based on Notion changes.

With this second scenario in place, any modifications you make to content within your Notion database will automatically trigger an update to the corresponding item on your Webflow website. This seamless synchronization ensures that your site always reflects the latest version of your content, making content management incredibly efficient and dynamic.

This two-scenario setup (one for creating new items and one for updating existing ones) forms a robust system for managing your Webflow site's content directly from Notion. It eliminates manual data entry and ensures consistency across both platforms.

Additional Advanced Steps

For those looking to integrate images dynamically into their Webflow site via Notion, there are a few advanced considerations. Notion's API, still in its beta phase, has certain limitations. One significant point to remember is that it does not currently include a dedicated "Image" type property. Therefore, to display images on your Webflow site, you must use a "URL" type property in your Notion database.

If you're sourcing images from the web, such as Google Images or Unsplash, you can typically obtain a direct URL by right-clicking the image and selecting "Copy Image Address." This direct URL can then be pasted into your Notion's 'Image' URL property. However, if your images are uploaded files, you'll need alternative methods to generate a usable URL.

How to obtain a direct image URL from a web source.

1. Dropbox

If you're accustomed to cloud storage services like Google Drive, the concept of creating a "shared" link in Dropbox for viewing or downloading files will be familiar. However, a standard Dropbox shared link isn't directly compatible with Webflow for displaying images.

To generate a direct image URL that Webflow can recognize, you need to modify the Dropbox shared link. Specifically, replace "dl=0" at the end of the URL with "raw=1". For example, a link like https://www.dropbox.com/s/c9buk5sd4mupfj8/Screen%20Shot%202021-08-23%20at%204.14.11%20PM.png?dl=0 would become https://www.dropbox.com/s/c9buk5sd4mupfj8/Screen%20Shot%202021-08-23%20at%204.14.11%20PM.png?raw=1. This small change makes the image directly embeddable.

Conveniently, this entire process can be automated within Make.com. By using the Dropbox module in conjunction with Make's Replace() text function, you can dynamically convert shared Dropbox links into Webflow-compatible direct image URLs as part of your automation workflow, eliminating the need for manual modification.

Automating Dropbox URL conversion with Make's Replace function.

2. Add a module that can generate a image URL for you

Another effective strategy for dynamically generating image URLs within your automation workflow is to leverage Make.com's social media modules. Platforms like Instagram, Twitter, or Reddit can be used to host images and provide direct URLs.

When you configure a Make scenario to "post" content with an image attachment to one of these social media sites, the module will often generate a public image URL. This URL can then be captured and used in your Notion database's 'Image' URL property, which subsequently feeds into Webflow.

For example, if you have a project where you post abstract art daily to a subreddit like /r/AbstractArt, you can set up an automation. When you post an image to Reddit via Make, the scenario can be designed to also create a new database item in Notion. The Reddit-generated image URL is then attached to this Notion item, ensuring it gets published on your Webflow site. This method provides a streamlined way to manage and display images that originate from social media activity.

Using a Reddit module in Make to generate and capture image URLs.

Pro tip: When using social media platforms to generate image URLs, be mindful of their terms of service and privacy settings. Ensure that the images are publicly accessible and that you have the necessary rights to use and display them on your website.

What This Workflow Does

Manually logging your comings and goings is tedious, error-prone, and often forgotten. Whether you're a field service technician tracking site visits, a remote employee logging work hours, or a business owner monitoring store visits, relying on memory or paper notes creates inaccurate records and administrative headaches.

This automation solves that by turning your iPhone into a passive tracking device. Using the Make iOS app, you define "geofence" circles around locations like your office, client sites, or home. Every time you enter or leave one of these circles, the workflow automatically triggers and creates a detailed log entry in your SeaTable database. This includes the timestamp, location name, and event type (entry or exit), building a perfect, auditable trail of your movements without you lifting a finger.

The business value is significant: it transforms location data from a manual chore into an automated asset. You gain accurate data for client billing, payroll, operational analysis, and compliance, while saving 30-60 minutes per person per week on manual logging tasks.

How It Works

The workflow acts as a bridge between your physical movement and your digital records. Here’s the step-by-step process:

Step 1: Geofence Trigger on Your iPhone

You set up location circles (geofences) in the Make iOS app for places you frequent. Your phone's GPS runs in the background. The moment you cross the virtual boundary of a defined circle, the Make app detects it and sends an instant webhook notification to your Make.com scenario.

Pro tip: Set geofence radii between 100-500 meters for reliable triggering without false positives from nearby roads or buildings.

Step 2: Data Processing in Make.com

The Make.com scenario receives the webhook payload containing key details: your device ID, the name of the location, the event type ("entered" or "left"), and the precise timestamp. Make.com formats this raw data into a clean, structured record ready for your database.

Step 3: Log Creation in SeaTable

Make.com connects to your SeaTable account and adds a new row to your designated table. The row is populated with the formatted data: Date & Time, Location, Event, and any additional custom fields you've configured, like "Employee Name" or "Project Code."

This entire chain—from GPS detection to database entry—happens within seconds, completely autonomously.

What You'll Need

To implement this workflow, you'll need to have the following accounts and tools ready:

  1. A Make.com account (free or paid tier) to build and run the automation scenario.
  2. The Make iOS app installed on your iPhone, with location permissions enabled.
  3. A SeaTable account (a free plan offers ample functionality for this use case).
  4. A SeaTable base with a table prepared to receive logs. Recommended columns: Timestamp (Date), Location (Text), Event Type (Single Select: "Entered", "Left"), and Notes (Long Text).
  5. Basic familiarity with connecting apps in Make.com, though the template provides the blueprint.

Quick Setup Guide

Follow these steps to get your location logging system up and running in under 20 minutes.

  1. Clone the Template: Click "Get This Workflow" to copy the scenario to your Make.com account.
  2. Configure the Make iOS Module: In the scenario, open the iOS "Watch Location" module. Use the Make app on your phone to define your geofence circles (e.g., "Main Office," "Client A Warehouse").
  3. Connect SeaTable: In the SeaTable module within Make.com, authorize the connection to your account and select your target base and table.
  4. Map the Data Fields: Ensure the data from the iOS trigger (location name, timestamp, event) is correctly mapped to the corresponding columns in your SeaTable table.
  5. Test and Activate: Turn on the scenario in Make.com. Physically enter one of your defined geofences and check SeaTable within a minute for a new "Entered" log. Once confirmed, the system is live.

Pro tip: Start with one or two key locations to test reliability. Once confident, add more geofences for other common sites.

What This Workflow Does

In many tech companies, a frustrating gap exists between developer tools and project management platforms. Engineers log bugs, features, and tasks in Redmine—a powerful issue tracker. Meanwhile, product managers, marketers, and executives track progress on visual Trello boards. This disconnect forces someone to manually copy information between systems, a process that is slow, error-prone, and creates information silos.

This Make.com workflow template solves that problem by creating an automatic bridge. Every time a developer creates a new issue in Redmine, this automation instantly triggers and creates a corresponding card on a specified Trello board. The card includes key details like the issue title, description, priority, and assignee. This means the entire team gains real-time visibility into development work without developers having to switch contexts or project managers having to chase for updates.

How It Works

The automation runs on a simple but powerful trigger-action principle, ensuring no issue falls through the cracks.

Step 1: Monitoring Redmine for New Issues

The workflow starts with a Make.com "Watch Issues" module connected to your Redmine account. It polls the Redmine API at regular intervals (e.g., every 15 minutes) to detect newly created issues. You can configure it to watch specific projects or trackers to keep the automation focused.

Step 2: Processing and Mapping Issue Data

Once a new issue is detected, Make.com captures its data. The workflow then processes this information, extracting the relevant fields. It can map Redmine's priority levels (e.g., High, Normal, Low) to corresponding Trello labels (red, yellow, green), and even look up the assignee's email to match them to a Trello member.

Pro tip: Use Make.com's filter to only create Trello cards for issues with a "High" or "Urgent" priority. This prevents your Trello board from being flooded and keeps it focused on critical items.

Step 3: Creating the Trello Card

The final step is the "Create a Card" module in Trello. Using the mapped data, the automation posts a new card to your chosen board and list (like "Backlog" or "To Do"). The card's name becomes the issue subject, and the description includes a link back to the original Redmine ticket for full context. The workflow is now complete, and the team can start working on the Trello card immediately.
